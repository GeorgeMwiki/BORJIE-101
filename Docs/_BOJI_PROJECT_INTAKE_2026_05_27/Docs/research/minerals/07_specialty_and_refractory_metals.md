## 1 · Tantalum (Ta) + Niobium (Nb) — the coltan twins

### A · Identity
- **Tantalum (Ta)**: atomic number 73, mass 180.95, density 16.65 g/cm³, melting point 3017 °C. Crustal abundance ~ 2 ppm.
- **Niobium (Nb)**: atomic number 41, mass 92.91, density 8.57 g/cm³, melting point 2477 °C. Crustal abundance ~ 17 ppm.
- Geochemically coherent (Group V transition metals); always co-occur but Nb is 5-10× more abundant.
- Principal ore minerals:
  - **Columbite (Fe,Mn)Nb₂O₆** + **Tantalite (Fe,Mn)Ta₂O₆** — solid-solution series; "coltan" trade name when mixed.
  - **Pyrochlore (Na,Ca)₂Nb₂O₆(OH,F)** — carbonatite-hosted, Nb-dominant.
  - **Microlite (Na,Ca)₂Ta₂O₆(OH,F)** — Ta-dominant pyrochlore-group.
  - **Euxenite, Samarskite, Fergusonite** — REE-Ta-Nb minerals.

### B · Geology & deposit types
- **LCT pegmatites (Lithium-Caesium-Tantalum)** — Greenbushes (WA), Pilgangoora, Wodgina, Manono (DRC), Marropino (Mozambique), Bald Hill, Tanco (Manitoba), Mibra (Brazil).
- **Carbonatite-hosted pyrochlore** for Nb — Araxá (Brazil, ~ 75% world Nb supply via **CBMM**), Catalão (Brazil, China Molybdenum), Niobec (Quebec, Magris Resources), Lueshe (DRC).
- **Granite-related apogranite** Russian deposits.

### C · Tanzania & East Africa relevance
- NW Tanzania (Kagera, Mara) — minor LCT pegmatite occurrences feeding into the **DRC/Rwanda/Burundi 3T (tin-tantalum-tungsten) ASM ecosystem**.
- Tanzanian operators may serve as transit/processing nodes for DRC coltan — **ITSCI tagging is non-negotiable**.
- **OECD Due Diligence Guidance** (3T/Gold/Annex II): Boji must enforce chain-of-custody on every tantalum sale.

### D · Exploration & determination
- **pXRF** field-screening of pegmatite outcrops for Ta/Nb anomalies.
- Pathfinder geochemistry: **Cs, Rb, Li, Be, Sn, F** for LCT pegmatites.
- **Gamma-spectrometry** (Th, U channels) — pyrochlore is often radioactive.
- **Lab analysis**: Ta/Nb by XRF or ICP-MS; **alkali fusion** (Na₂O₂ or LiBO₂) required for total dissolution; specific-gravity test (SG > 6.5 for coltan vs ~ 2.7 for quartz host).
- Bulk-sample gravity test on Wilfley or Mozley to confirm metallurgical recovery.

### E · Mining
- Pegmatite: open-pit or hand-cobbing for narrow dykes; some narrow-vein UG.
- Pyrochlore carbonatite: large-scale open-pit (Araxá strip mine).
- **ASM coltan in DRC/Rwanda**: hand-dug pits, manual sluicing in streams; major human-rights flag (Pact, RMI, IPIS reports).

### F · Beneficiation
- **Gravity** dominates: cassiterite-style — shaking tables (Wilfley, Holman), spirals, jigs, MGS for fines.
- **Magnetic separation**: WHIMS removes columbite-tantalite from non-magnetic gangue (Fe-Mn columbite is paramagnetic).
- **Electrostatic separation**: separates Ta-Nb minerals (conductors) from zircon (non-conductor).
- **Flotation** rarely used due to gravity-amenability.
- Concentrate typically **20-40% combined Ta₂O₅ + Nb₂O₅**; coltan industry-standard sale unit is **"%Ta₂O₅"** since Ta is more valuable than Nb.

### G · Refining → Ta and Nb metal
- **Cracking**: concentrate digested in **conc. HF + H₂SO₄ at 90 °C** → soluble fluorocomplexes of Ta and Nb.
- **Solvent extraction (MIBK)**: separates Ta from Nb (Ta extracts into MIBK at low HF concentration; Nb extracts at higher HF). The classic Sutulov / Bauer separation.
- **Precipitation**:
  - Ta as **K₂TaF₇** (potassium heptafluorotantalate) → trade product for further reduction.
  - Nb as Nb₂O₅ or ammonium niobium fluoride.
- **Metal production**:
  - Ta: K₂TaF₇ + Na → Ta powder (sodium reduction); or carbothermic reduction of Ta₂O₅.
  - Nb: aluminothermic reduction (Nb₂O₅ + Al → Nb + Al₂O₃) or carbothermic.
- **Ferroniobium (FeNb 65-70%)** for steel: pyrochlore + Fe + reductant in submerged-arc furnace; CBMM's specialty.

### H · Final products & specifications
- **K₂TaF₇** — Ta source for downstream.
- **Ta metal powder** (capacitor grade, > 99.95%, "C-grade" for capacitors).
- **Nb₂O₅** — > 99.5% for super-alloys.
- **FeNb 65** — steel additive (HSLA — high-strength low-alloy steel; pipeline, automotive).
- **Nb metal** — superconductor magnets (Nb-Ti, Nb₃Sn) for MRI, particle accelerators.

### I · Pricing
- **Ta**: Asian Metal, Argus — Ta₂O₅ concentrate USD 80-120/lb (200-300/kg).
- **K₂TaF₇**: derivatives premium.
- **Nb**: ferroniobium FOB Brazil USD 35-50/kg Nb content; ~ 85% world supply controlled by **CBMM** + Niobec.
- **Capacitor-grade Ta powder**: USD 200-400/kg.

### J · EHS & due diligence
- **3T conflict minerals** — Dodd-Frank §1502 + EU Conflict Minerals Regulation: every downstream company (Apple, Samsung, Intel, AMD, Tesla) requires ITSCI / RMAP chain-of-custody.
- **Radioactivity**: pyrochlore often Th/U-bearing — NORM disposal regulation.
- **HF use** in refining — extremely hazardous; specialist licensing.

### K · Decision triggers for Boji
- **Coltan in NW TZ / cross-border DRC**: **REQUIRE ITSCI tag system + RMAP audit before any export**; otherwise refuse to advise sale.
- **Pyrochlore prospect**: carbonatite-hosted Nb is high-capex; only viable at CBMM scale; flag JV pattern.
- **HF refining**: ASM/SME cannot operate; export concentrate as Ta₂O₅ — do not chase downstream.
- **Capacitor-grade vs commodity Ta**: capacitor-grade demands 5N purity; technical-grade for super-alloys.
- **Penalty elements**: U₃O₈ > 0.5%, ThO₂ > 0.5% triggers NORM regulatory pathway.

## 2 · Antimony (Sb)

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 51, mass 121.76, density 6.69 g/cm³, melting point 631 °C; brittle metalloid.
- **Stibnite Sb₂S₃** — primary ore; ~ 71% Sb; needle-like silvery-grey crystals.
- **Senarmontite Sb₂O₃**, **valentinite Sb₂O₃**, **kermesite Sb₂S₂O** — supergene.
- Often co-occurs with Au (Au-Sb veins — Slovakia, Italy, Mexico, China, AUS); ~10% of Au mines produce Sb as by-product.

### B · Geology
- Hydrothermal vein deposits, mesothermal to epithermal.
- China dominant (Hunan, Guizhou, Yunnan ~ 60% world supply); Tajikistan, Russia, Bolivia, Mexico, Australia, Turkey also producers.
- Au-Sb associations: Ballarat (AUS), Costerfield (AUS), Murchison (AUS).

### C · Tanzania
- Limited; some minor stibnite occurrences but no commercial deposit.

### D-F · Mining & beneficiation
- Underground vein mining typical (narrow stibnite vein widths).
- **Gravity** (jig, table) for coarse stibnite; **flotation** with xanthate (PAX, SIPX) at pH 4-6 for fines.
- Concentrate **55-65% Sb** typical.

### G · Refining
- **Volatilisation roast**: stibnite Sb₂S₃ + O₂ → **Sb₂O₃ volatile fume** captured as condensed dust in bag-house; SO₂ off-gas.
- **Reduction**: Sb₂O₃ + C → Sb metal (regulus); cast as ingot.
- **Refining**: pyrometallurgical (drossing for As, Pb removal) + electrorefining for high purity.
- **Sodium antimonate** (NaSbO₃) for flame-retardant additive; **Sb₂O₃** for flame-retardant + PET catalyst.

### H · Hazard, movement
- Sb metal: not DG in bulk.
- **Sb₂O₃**: UN 1549, marine pollutant; inhalation toxic (carcinogen IARC 2B).
- Sb-As co-occurrence raises worker-exposure flag.

### I · Pricing
- **Fastmarkets / MMTA**: Antimony 99.65% min in-warehouse Rotterdam — historically USD 5,000-15,000/t; 2024-2025 saw multi-year highs due to China supply constraints.
- **China export controls** (Aug 2024) restricted Sb exports — major price spike (USD 25,000+ /t late 2024).

### J · EHS
- Sb dust / fume inhalation toxic.
- Often paired with As (stibnite + arsenopyrite) — extra worker-exposure flag.

### K · Decision triggers
- **Au mine with Sb credit > 1%**: model Sb separately; refining route depends on whether refinery accepts Sb-bearing dore (penalty for Sb in Au).
- **China export-control regulatory tracking**: TZ Sb owner has supply-security opportunity post-2024.

### L · Sources
- [USGS Antimony Statistics](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/national-minerals-information-center/antimony-statistics-and-information)
- [Fastmarkets Antimony](https://www.fastmarkets.com/antimony/)
- [Minor Metals Trade Association](https://mmta.co.uk/)

## 3 · Bismuth (Bi)

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 83, mass 208.98, density 9.78 g/cm³, melting point 271.4 °C (low — "fusible metal").
- **Bismuthinite Bi₂S₃** — primary ore.
- **Bismite Bi₂O₃**, **bismutite Bi₂(CO₃)O₂** — oxide.
- Mostly **by-product of Pb refining** (drossing residues, Parkes process); some primary in Bolivia (Tasna), Mexico, Peru.

### B-F · Beneficiation & refining
- As Pb by-product: drossing residues → vacuum distillation → Bi metal (99.99%).
- Primary: gravity + flotation similar to Pb-Sb.

### G · Final products
- **Bi metal ingot / shot / powder** ≥ 99.99%.
- **Low-melt alloys** (Wood's metal Bi-Pb-Sn-Cd, Field's metal Bi-In-Sn) — fusible safety links, soldering.
- **Bismuth oxychloride (BiOCl)** — cosmetic pearl pigment.
- **Bismuth subnitrate / subsalicylate** — pharmaceutical (Pepto-Bismol).
- **Lead-free pewter, brass replacement** — RoHS-driven demand.

### H-I · Hazard / pricing
- Not DG in bulk metal form; relatively non-toxic.
- Fastmarkets Bi 99.99% min in-warehouse Rotterdam — USD 4-15/lb historically; ~ USD 7-12 typical 2025.

### J-K · EHS / decision triggers
- One of the few "green" heavy metals — lead replacement growth.
- TZ owner with Pb-Zn-Ag-Bi concentrate: Bi credit usually built into smelter offtake; SME cannot independently recover.

### L · Sources
- [USGS Bismuth Statistics](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/national-minerals-information-center/bismuth-statistics-and-information)
- [Fastmarkets Bismuth](https://www.fastmarkets.com/bismuth/)

## 4 · Tellurium (Te) and Selenium (Se) — Cu refining by-products

### A · Identity
- **Tellurium**: Z=52, mass 127.60, density 6.24, melting 449.5 °C. Crustal abundance ~ 0.001 ppm (rare).
- **Selenium**: Z=34, mass 78.96, density 4.81, melting 220 °C.
- Both occur in:
  - Cu anode slime (electrorefining residue) — primary commercial source.
  - Some refractory gold ores.
  - Sediment-hosted Cu (Kupferschiefer) for Se.
  - Calaverite AuTe₂, sylvanite (Au,Ag)Te₂ — Te + Au.

### B · Production routes
- **From Cu anode slime**: alkaline-leach → redox precipitation:
  - Selenium: SeO₂ → reduced with SO₂ → Se metal (red/grey allotrope).
  - Tellurium: TeO₃²⁻ → reduced with SO₂ → Te metal.
- **From Au refractory ore**: pressure-oxidation residues may carry Te; recovered as a credit.

### C · End-use markets
- **Tellurium → CdTe thin-film solar cells (First Solar dominant consumer)** — single-source demand driver; metallurgical Te in steel.
- **Selenium → glass decolorising (red glass), pigments, animal feed**, photoreceptors, electronics.

### D · Pricing
- Fastmarkets / Asian Metal — Te 99.99% USD 50-200/kg; Se 99.5% USD 8-30/kg; volatile thin markets.

### E · TZ relevance
- Not produced primarily in TZ; any Cu by-product credit goes to overseas refiners. SME cannot extract independently.

## 5 · Gallium (Ga) and Germanium (Ge) — China export-control story

### A · Identity
- **Gallium**: Z=31, mass 69.72, density 5.91 g/cm³, melting 29.8 °C (liquid in hand). Crustal abundance ~ 19 ppm.
- **Germanium**: Z=32, mass 72.63, density 5.32 g/cm³, melting 938.3 °C. Crustal abundance ~ 1.6 ppm.

### B · Production routes
- **Gallium**: from **Bayer-process spent liquor** (in alumina production); SX or electrochemical extraction; ~ 50 ppm Ga in bauxite. Also from Zn refining residues.
- **Germanium**: from **Zn refining cement** (Ge cements out on Zn dust); from coal fly ash (China); some primary in argyrodite Ag₈GeS₆.

### C · End-use markets
- **GaAs, GaN** — semiconductors, LEDs, 5G/6G RF, EV inverter power electronics, lasers.
- **Ge**: optical fibre, infrared optics (military thermal-imaging), polymerisation catalyst (PET bottles), semiconductors.
- Both: **China export-control regulations (Aug 2023)** — restricted Ga and Ge exports — massive supply-security flag; non-Chinese alumina refineries (Korea, Japan, Europe) ramped Ga recovery.

### D · Pricing
- Fastmarkets, Asian Metal — Ga metal 99.99% USD 300-800/kg; Ga metal 99.9999% (semiconductor) USD 1,000-3,000/kg; Ge metal USD 1,500-4,000/kg; both spiked 2-3× after Aug-2023 China controls.

### E · TZ relevance
- No bauxite refining in TZ → no Ga production.
- No Zn smelter → no Ge production.
- TZ critical-minerals policy *could* incentivise downstream Ga/Ge extraction from Cu/Zn anode slime if Cu refinery is built — speculative.

- [USGS Germanium](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/national-minerals-information-center/germanium-statistics-and-information)

## 6 · Indium (In)

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 49, mass 114.82, density 7.31 g/cm³, melting 156.6 °C; soft silvery metal.
- Crustal abundance ~ 0.05 ppm.
- Recovered as **by-product of Zn refining** (sphalerite typically contains 0.001-0.1% In, recoverable from residues).

### B · Production
- From Zn refining residues (zinc dust cement, jarosite/goethite residue) → acid leach → SX or cementation on Al → refining to 99.99% (4N) or 99.9999% (6N).

### C · End-use markets
- **Indium-Tin Oxide (ITO)** transparent conductive coatings on touchscreens, LCDs, OLEDs — dominant demand (~ 60% of supply).
- **CIGS (Cu-In-Ga-Se) thin-film solar cells**.
- **In-Sn solders**, In-Pb low-melt alloys.
- **Compound semiconductors** (InP, InGaAs).

### D · Pricing
- Fastmarkets In 99.99% in-warehouse Rotterdam — USD 200-1,000/kg historically; sensitive to LCD/touchscreen demand cycle.

### E · TZ relevance
- Recovered only at primary Zn smelters; not feasible at SME scale.

## 7 · Beryllium (Be) — extreme hazard

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 4, mass 9.012, density 1.85 g/cm³ (very light), melting 1287 °C; high stiffness-to-weight ratio.
- Crustal abundance ~ 2.8 ppm.
- **Beryl Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈** — primary ore (also the gem variety: emerald with Cr, aquamarine, morganite, heliodor).
- **Bertrandite Be₄Si₂O₇(OH)₂** — Spor Mountain, Utah (US near-monopoly).

### B · Geology
- Pegmatite (beryl crystals embedded in granitic pegmatite) — Brazil, Madagascar, Mozambique, Tanzania, Afghanistan, USA.
- Bertrandite-bearing tuff: Spor Mountain (Utah, Materion) — sole large producer.

### C · TZ relevance
- Tanzania has beryl-bearing pegmatites in **Morogoro region (Manyara emerald belt and gem-quality beryl)**; commercial bertrandite extraction not yet established.
- Most TZ beryl ends up in the gemstone market, not the metal market (see file `05_gemstones_and_diamonds.md`).

### D-F · Mining & beneficiation
- Hand-cobbing of large beryl crystals from pegmatite.
- For bertrandite: sulphuric-acid leach.
- For beryl-to-metal: fluoride extraction or chloride process.

### G · Refining → metal
- Complex acid leach + solvent extraction → Be(OH)₂ → BeF₂ → reduction with Mg → Be metal.
- Only **~ 3 plants worldwide** (Materion USA, Ulba Kazakhstan, NGK Japan).

### H · Hazard — extreme
- **Chronic Beryllium Disease (CBD)** — progressive, irreversible lung disease from microgram-level inhalation; OSHA PEL 0.2 µg/m³; medical surveillance mandatory.
- **UN 1567** — Beryllium powder Class 6.1 Toxic Solid PG II.
- **BeO ceramic dust** equally hazardous.

### I · Pricing
- Closed-market specialty; Materion controls pricing; Be metal ~ USD 600-1,000/kg; copper-beryllium alloy widely used in aerospace.

### J · End-use markets
- Aerospace components (lightweight high-stiffness).
- **X-ray windows** (Be is transparent to X-rays).
- **Copper-beryllium alloys** (springs, non-sparking tools, electrical contacts).
- **BeO ceramics** (high-thermal-conductivity, electrical-insulator).
- Nuclear reactor neutron moderator/reflector.

### K · Decision triggers
- **TZ pegmatite owner with beryl**: route to **gemstone market** (emerald, aquamarine) — far more profitable than metal extraction.
- **Bertrandite extraction**: refuse small-operator advice; Materion or licensed processor only — Be metal is one of the most regulated minerals globally.

- [Materion Corporation](https://materion.com/)

## 8 · Hafnium (Hf)

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 72, mass 178.49, density 13.31 g/cm³, melting 2233 °C.
- Crustal abundance ~ 3.0 ppm.
- **Always co-occurs with Zr** in zircon (ZrSiO₄ contains 1-3% Hf by weight); recovered during Zr nuclear-grade purification.

### B · Production
- From Hf-Zr separation by **solvent extraction with HSCN/MIBK** during nuclear-grade Zr production (Hf has very high neutron-capture cross-section vs Zr — they MUST be separated for nuclear cladding).
- Cycle: Zr/Hf chloride → SX → Hf-depleted Zr (nuclear-grade) + Hf concentrate → calcine to HfO₂ → reduce to metal.

### C · End-use markets
- **Super-alloys** for jet engines and gas turbines (single-crystal blade additive at 1-2% Hf).
- **Plasma cutting electrodes**.
- **Nuclear control rods** (high neutron-capture).
- **Semiconductor gate dielectric** (HfO₂ replaces SiO₂ in advanced node CMOS).

### D · Pricing
- Specialty traders (Tradium, Avon Metals, ATI Allegheny) — USD 1,500-4,000/kg Hf metal.
- Tiny market (~ 50-70 t/yr globally).

### E · TZ relevance
- Recovered only at nuclear-grade Zr refineries; not feasible in TZ.

## 9 · Scandium (Sc)

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 21, mass 44.96, density 2.99 g/cm³, melting 1541 °C.
- Crustal abundance ~ 22 ppm (more common than Pb, but rarely concentrated).
- No single dominant ore mineral; recovered as by-product from:
  - **Red mud** (Bayer-process bauxite residue, 100-150 ppm Sc).
  - **Ni laterite** processing residues (CCN Mining Yunnan, Indonesia HPAL).
  - **TiO₂ pigment plant** residues.
  - **Uranium leach residues**.

### B · Production routes
- Solvent extraction from acidic process streams → Sc₂O₃ → metal by Ca/Al reduction.

### C · End-use markets
- **Sc-Al alloy** (Al-0.1-0.5% Sc) — aerospace, sporting goods; very stiff but very expensive.
- **SOFC (Solid Oxide Fuel Cell) electrolyte** (Sc-stabilised zirconia).
- **High-intensity mercury-vapour lamps**.

### D · Pricing
- USD 1,500-2,500/kg Sc₂O₃; USD 5,000-10,000/kg Sc metal; tiny market (~ 20-30 t/yr globally).
- Rusal (Russia), Sumitomo (Japan), Yunnan (China) are the main producers.

### E · TZ relevance
- No production; potential by-product from any future TZ HPAL Ni operation or REE processing.

## 11 · Chromium (Cr)

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 24, mass 52.00, density 7.19 g/cm³, melting 1907 °C.
- **Chromite FeCr₂O₄** — sole commercial ore; spinel-group mineral with variable Mg/Fe and Al/Cr substitution.
- Cr/Fe ratio is the key quality metric (higher Cr/Fe → premium for stainless-steel feed; lower → metallurgical/chemical/refractory).

### B · Geology
- **Stratiform** layered intrusions: Bushveld Complex (South Africa, ~ 75% world reserves), Great Dyke (Zimbabwe), Stillwater (Montana).
- **Podiform / ophiolite-hosted**: Albania, Turkey, Greece, Oman, Philippines, New Caledonia, Iran.

### C · TZ relevance
- Limited; some ultramafic outcrops in NW + Mozambique-Belt edges but no commercial chromite deposit.

### D-F · Mining & beneficiation
- Stratiform: large-scale open-pit (Bushveld LG6, MG, UG1/2 reefs); some UG (UG2 in Bushveld — also a PGM feed).
- Podiform: small-scale UG / open-pit.
- **DMS (heavy-media separation)** for coarse chromite.
- **Gravity** (spirals, jigs) for fines.
- **Magnetic separation** (chromite is paramagnetic at high field).
- **Flotation** rarely used; can use fatty-acid collector at pH 2-3 (acid conditions).

### G · Smelting → ferrochrome
- **Submerged-arc furnace** (SAF): chromite + coke + flux → ferrochrome alloy + slag at 1700 °C.
- Grades:
  - **HC FeCr (High-Carbon)** — 60-70% Cr, 6-8% C; cheapest, main stainless-steel feed.
  - **MC FeCr (Medium-Carbon)** — 1-3% C.
  - **LC FeCr (Low-Carbon)** — < 1% C; for low-C stainless and tool steel.
  - **Charge chrome** — Bushveld-standard 50-55% Cr (lower than HC FeCr).
- **Chemical chromium**: Na₂Cr₂O₇ (sodium dichromate) → CrO₃ → leather tanning, plating, pigment.
- **Refractory chromite**: as raw chromite for steelmaking refractory bricks.

### H · Movement / pricing
- Chromite ore bulk vessel; ferrochrome super-sack or bulk.
- Pricing: **Fastmarkets FeCr 65% Cr / 0.10% C** — basis pricing per Cr unit; benchmark Chinese stainless-steel market sets quarterly.
- China is largest consumer; SA is largest producer.

### I · EHS
- **Cr(VI) is highly toxic + carcinogen** (Erin Brockovich case); modern smelting controls.
- Smelter SO₂ + NO_x emissions.
- Ferrochrome slag — beneficial use as cement extender / aggregate increasingly.

### J · Decision triggers
- Bushveld-class reserves dominate world supply; small operator cannot compete economically.
- TZ ultramafic owner with chromite showing: model as JV-with-Bushveld-major or refuse to advise standalone development.

### K · Sources
- [International Chromium Development Association](https://www.icdacr.com/)
- [USGS Chromium Statistics](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/national-minerals-information-center/chromium-statistics-and-information)
- [Fastmarkets Ferrochrome](https://www.fastmarkets.com/ferrochrome/)

## 12 · Molybdenum (Mo)

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 42, mass 95.95, density 10.28 g/cm³, melting 2623 °C.
- **Molybdenite MoS₂** — sole commercial ore; soft hexagonal mineral resembling graphite but heavier.
- Crustal abundance ~ 1.2 ppm.

### B · Geology
- **Cu-Mo porphyry** by-product — Bingham (Utah), Chuquicamata, Sierrita, La Caridad. ~ 60% of world Mo from Cu by-product.
- **Primary Mo porphyry** — Climax (Colorado), Henderson (Colorado), Mt Emmons. ~ 40% of supply.
- Skarn (Cadia, Cantung).

### C · TZ relevance
- Minor Cu-Mo prospects but no commercial Mo deposit.

### D-F · Mining & beneficiation
- Co-floated with Cu by-product in porphyry concentrators:
  - Bulk Cu-Mo float.
  - Mo cleaner circuit at very high pH (12+) with NaSH to depress Cu — molybdenite floats with diesel/kerosene collector + MIBC frother.
  - Mo concentrate **48-58% Mo**.

### G · Refining
- **Oxidative roast** of MoS₂ → MoO₃ (sublimes at 1155 °C, condensed as "technical-grade MoO₃"); SO₂ off-gas.
- **Ferromolybdenum (FeMo)**: thermite reaction of MoO₃ + Fe₂O₃ + Si/Al reductant → 60-70% Mo alloy for steel.
- **Pure MoO₃** further sublimation/refining for chemical use.
- **Mo metal powder**: H₂ reduction of MoO₃.

### H · End-use markets
- **HSLA and stainless steel** alloy (improves hardenability, corrosion resistance).
- **Lubricants** (MoS₂ as solid lubricant in extreme conditions).
- **Catalysts** (HDS — hydrodesulphurisation in oil refining).
- **Cemented carbide** (with W).

### I · Pricing
- **Fastmarkets MoO₃ FOB Platts** — daily oxide pricing; USD 8-25/lb Mo content.
- **FeMo 65% FOB**: USD 18-50/kg Mo content.

### J · Decision triggers
- Standalone Mo-only mining: Climax-class capex only.
- TZ Cu porphyry owner with Mo by-product (e.g., future Mpanda Cu): model Mo separately; offtake to Mo-converter (Freeport's Climax, ICDA member).

### K · Sources
- [International Molybdenum Association](https://www.imoa.info/)
- [USGS Molybdenum Statistics](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/national-minerals-information-center/molybdenum-statistics-and-information)

## 13 · Titanium (Ti) and Zirconium (Zr) — HMS family

> See file `08_construction_and_heavy_mineral_sands.md` for Tanzania HMS context. This section focuses on metallurgy.

### A · Titanium identity
- Atomic number 22, mass 47.87, density 4.51 g/cm³, melting 1668 °C; highest strength-to-weight ratio of common metals.
- Principal ore minerals:
  - **Ilmenite FeTiO₃** — 53% TiO₂ theoretical; dominant feedstock.
  - **Rutile TiO₂** — 95-99% TiO₂; premium chloride-process feed.
  - **Leucoxene** — Ilmenite altered to higher-TiO₂ form (60-75% TiO₂).
  - **Anatase TiO₂** — sedimentary; Brazil Tapira.
  - **Titanomagnetite** — magnetite with 5-15% TiO₂ (Liganga TZ).

### B · Zirconium identity
- Atomic number 40, mass 91.22, density 6.51, melting 1855 °C.
- **Zircon ZrSiO₄** — 67% ZrO₂; tetragonal; UV-fluorescent; very stable; ~ 1-3% Hf substitution always present.
- **Baddeleyite ZrO₂** — minor primary (Phalaborwa SA).

### C · Tanzania
- HMS placer beach sand: **Pwani-Mtwara coast** — Strandline Resources / Tajiri / Fungoni projects (see file 08).
- Titanomagnetite at **Liganga** (Mbeya).

### D-F · HMS beneficiation
- Wet plant: spirals → HMC (Heavy Mineral Concentrate, ~ 95% heavies).
- Dry plant: heat-dry → magnetic separation (ilmenite reports magnetic; rutile + zircon report non-magnetic) → electrostatic separation (rutile + ilmenite are conductors; zircon is non-conductor).
- Final products: separate ilmenite, rutile, leucoxene, zircon, monazite (NORM, often rejected/stockpiled), garnet sand.

### G · Titanium metallurgy
- **Sulphate process** (older, ilmenite feed): ilmenite + H₂SO₄ → TiOSO₄ → hydrolyse → TiO₂ pigment + FeSO₄ + waste acid (limited by waste acid disposal).
- **Chloride process** (newer, rutile/synthetic-rutile feed): rutile + C + Cl₂ → TiCl₄ + CO₂; TiCl₄ purified by distillation → oxidised back to TiO₂ pigment OR reduced to Ti metal.
- **Kroll process** (Ti metal): TiCl₄ + Mg → Ti sponge + MgCl₂; sponge ground, alloyed, vacuum-arc-remelted → Ti metal ingot.
- **Becher / Benilite / Murso processes** (synthetic rutile): ilmenite reduction + Fe removal → 92% TiO₂ feedstock for chloride process.

### H · Zirconium metallurgy
- **Caustic decomposition**: zircon + NaOH at 600 °C → soluble sodium zirconate + sodium silicate.
- **Acid digestion**: HCl + H₂SO₄ → ZrOCl₂.
- **Zr-Hf separation**: solvent extraction with HSCN/MIBK or ion-exchange (nuclear-grade requires Hf < 100 ppm).
- **Kroll process**: ZrCl₄ + Mg → Zr sponge → vacuum-arc-remelt → metal ingot.
- **Ceramic-grade zircon flour**: ZrSiO₄ ground to D50 5-10 µm, sold directly (no metal conversion); 70%+ of zircon market.

### I · End-use markets
- **TiO₂ pigment (~ 90% of Ti demand)**: paint, plastic, paper, cosmetics, sunscreen (Tronox, Chemours, Kronos, Iluka, Venator).
- **Ti metal (Ti-6Al-4V dominant alloy)**: aerospace, medical implants, military, sporting; ~ USD 7-12/kg sponge to USD 50-200/kg finished alloy.
- **Zircon flour**: ceramics (tile opacifier, glaze), refractories, foundry, abrasives.
- **Zr metal**: **nuclear cladding** (Hf-free, low neutron-capture), chemical industry corrosion-resistance.
- **Zirconia ZrO₂** ceramics: dental crowns, oxygen sensors, SOFC, thermal-barrier coatings.

### J · Pricing
- **Ilmenite** USD 200-400/t FOB Australia (sulphate-grade); USD 350-600/t (synthetic-rutile equivalent).
- **Rutile** USD 1,300-1,900/t FOB (chloride premium).
- **Zircon** USD 1,500-2,500/t bulk.
- **TiO₂ pigment** USD 2,500-4,000/t.
- **Ti sponge** USD 7-12/kg.
- TZMI, Fastmarkets HMS indices.

### K · EHS
- **TiO₂ pigment IARC Group 2B** (possibly carcinogenic via inhalation of nano-particles) — controversial; EU food-additive ban 2022.
- HMS coastal-mining rehabilitation obligations.
- NORM (monazite Th) — IAEA-equivalent disposal mandatory.

### L · Decision triggers
- TZ HMS owner: market matched to product:
  - Sulphate-grade ilmenite → Chinese / Eastern-European pigment producers.
  - Chloride-grade rutile → Tronox / Chemours / Iluka.
  - Zircon flour → ceramic industry (Italy, Spain, China tile clusters).
- Monazite fraction: NORM regulatory; consider rejection vs storage vs REE-feed sale to Iluka / Lynas.
- Ti metal sponge: not feasible in TZ (Kroll process scale + capex prohibitive).

## 14 · Mercury (Hg) — Minamata-constrained, lawful-only treatment

### A · Identity
- Atomic number 80, mass 200.59, density **13.534 g/cm³** (liquid at room temperature), boiling 356.7 °C.
- **Cinnabar HgS** — primary ore (red); historically Almadén (Spain), Idrija (Slovenia), Huancavelica (Peru).
- **Native mercury Hg** — minor; ore-roasting residue or seepage.

### B · Geology
- Hydrothermal vein deposits in young volcanic / metamorphic terrains.
- Major historical sources: Spain (Almadén, ~ 250,000 tonnes lifetime; closed 2003), Slovenia (Idrija, closed), China (Tongren, still operating), Kyrgyzstan (Khaidarkan), Algeria.

### C · TZ context — **import-side regulation only**
- Tanzania has **no commercial Hg deposits**.
- Hg is **imported for ASGM (Artisanal Small-Scale Gold Mining)** — amalgamation.
- Tanzania **signed the Minamata Convention on Mercury (10 Oct 2013, ratified 16 Aug 2017)**.
- **National Action Plan (NAP) 2020-2025** committed to phase out mercury use in ASGM (per the IIED report and NAP document; see file `01_TZ_MINING_REGULATION_2025_2026.md`).
- Estimated TZ ASGM mercury consumption **13.2-24.4 tonnes/year** (slow implementation).
- **Boji's role is strictly compliance + abatement** — never advise commercial Hg use that increases exposure.

### D-G · Lawful compliance scope only
- **Retort use** — gold amalgam vapourised in a closed vessel; Hg vapour recovered; 95%+ recovery; protects worker and environment.
- **Banded washing area** — concrete-lined containment with recovery sump; prevents Hg contamination of soil/water.
- **Mercury-free alternatives**:
  - **Borax direct-smelt** (Philippine/Indonesian method): coarse-gold concentrates direct-smelted with borax flux at 1100 °C → gold button + slag; eliminates Hg entirely.
  - **Glycine leach** (Mintek Igoli, EcoGold): non-toxic alternative to cyanide AND mercury.
  - **Gravity-only flowsheet** (Knelson + sluice + shaking table): for coarse-gold liberation; eliminates Hg need.
  - **Cyanide CIL/CIP** (regulated): industrial-scale alternative; not for ASM.

### H · Hazard
- **UN 2809** — Mercury, Class 8 PG III.
- **Methylmercury** in food chain (Minamata Bay disease, Japan 1950s); Tanzanian downstream waterways at risk.
- Worker inhalation: ataxia, tremors, neurological damage.
- Children + pregnant women: developmental neurotoxicity.

### I · Trade — Minamata-restricted
- Import requires prior informed consent (PIC) under Minamata.
- Export restricted; PIC procedure mandatory.

### J · Decision triggers for Boji
- **REFUSE** any commercial mercury extraction advice.
- **REFUSE** any operational advice that increases Hg exposure.
- **REQUIRE** retort + banded-washing-area + worker PPE for any legacy gold operation using Hg.
- **ADVOCATE** mercury-free alternatives (borax, glycine, gravity-only).
- **TRACK** the operator's Minamata compliance status as part of the EPP / EIA review.
